CellGrow: growing cells in a computer


      During my hour+ long train commute to my workplace (thankfully mostly only twice a week), I mostly read. My favourite topics have been philosophy, biology, neuroscience, history and historical biographies. Three books on biology that I have read within the last year are The Self-Assembling Brain: How Neural Networks Grow Smarter by Peter Robin Hiesinger, How Life Works: A User’s Guide to the New Biology by Philip Ball, and The Demon in the Machine: How Hidden Webs of Information Are Finally Solving the Mystery of Life by Paul Davies. Although they present different topics of biology, these authors agree on one thing: cells and cellular development is incredibly complex and we are still very far from truly understanding how life works. While Paul Davies posits that a new approach and paradigm that wholly incorporates information into the physics of life is required to move forward in understanding life, Peter Robin Hiesinger asks how is it possible that a single fertilised egg contains enough information to recreate a whole organism, focusing specifically on the development of neurons and targeted synapses, which seem to require much more information than could be contained within a sinlge cell. Hiesinger gives a striking example of cell-encoded information through targeted synapse develoment in the animal kingdom: Monarch butterfly migration. These butterflies return to a few square kilometers of forest in Mexico, after having migrated through multiple generations (!) to California. The generation that migrates to this very specific place in Mexico has never been there, and not even their grandparents have been. How is it possible, then, that they have such precise information on the whereabouts of this forest? Clearly, this information about the whereabouts of this forest and the path to get there, has to be present, in a very compacted way, in the fertilised egg of the Monarch butterfly.

Inspired by these incredible facts and new ways of thinking about life and cellular development as presented in these books, I wanted to see whether I could come up with a toy model of cellular growth that could lead to morphological complexity from simple rules, similar to the information unfolding that goes on in biological cells. Unbeknowst to me until this point, but not suprising, there is a whole field of study focusing precisely on this problem, as presented in these recent review articles: Agent-based models in cellular systems, and Cell-Based Computational Models of Organoids: A Systematic Review.

My aim with this model was mostly to have fun coding it and to see how far can I get with my simple approach. And of course to get some pretty pictures and animations. The script, written in Python and available on my GitHub, performs a clear goal: starting from a single cell, which are represented as little spheres of fixed radius, follow some simple rules defining cell division, stasis, and apoptosis, in a step-wise evolution in continous 3D space.

Algorithm description

      With every iteration, all cells (represented only through a single point in 3D space in the memory) are looped through and checked whether they should divide, stay, or die. There are multiple parameters that control this behaviour. If cells get too crowded, above the division threshold (quantified as the number of neighbouring cells within a specified radius), division is halted. If crowding becomes even higher, cells can also be instructed to 'die', or deleted from the list of cells. If cells are allowed to divide, two cells are created from the parent cell; placed at a specified distance, another parameter of the model.

The division of cells opens up the first important algorithmic decisions to be taken: one, along which direction should the cells divide and two, how do we avoid overlapping cells?
The directionality of division is an important generator of structure for the growing cell mass, and it is inspired by the biological phenomenon of cell polarity. Naturally, directionality only makes sense once there are multiple cells. Thus, in order to make a decision on directionality relative to other cells, we calculate for each cell a position vector, representing the sum of vectors to neighbouring cells. The magnitude and direction of this position vector determines two things, respectively: whether the cell is on the surface or inside the local cell mass and, which direction is towards the local 'interior' of the cell mass. By controlling the division direction with respect to this position vector, we can instruct cells to divide 'tangentially' to the local cell mass, that is, along the cell surface, or 'radially' away from the cell mass.

What about overlap? Using the position vector, we can partially avoid overlap, however, in general, it is not avoidable. Therefore, after cell division, an iterative relataxion step is required that pushes cells away if there is an overlap. The same relaxation step can also be used to enforce cell adhesion, or in other words, cells 'sticking' together. That is, applying an attractive force to cells that are too far apart. For relaxation Hooke's law is used, which can be thought of as having springs acting between cells that push or pull neighbouring cells to the division split distance.

An important numerical aspect of applying these cell growth rules is to keep neighbour operations fast as the colony grows. In CellGrow, this is done with a uniform-grid spatial hash: each cell is inserted into a 3D grid bucket and, for every query, only the 27 adjacent buckets are scanned. This keeps neighbour searches local and shifts the dominant per-step work from naive all-pairs \(\mathcal{O}(N^2)\) to approximately \(\mathcal{O}(k \cdot N)\), where \(k\) is the average local neighbour count. The simulation state itself is stored in CuPy arrays on GPU memory, and heavy local operations (neighbour-resultant accumulation, density counting, and spring/overlap relaxation) are executed through CUDA raw kernels, which removes most Python-loop overhead. Distances and vectors are still evaluated continuously in Cartesian space (the grid is only an acceleration structure, not a discretization of motion). In practice, this combination of grid-based candidate pruning + GPU kernels is what enables interactive-scale runs from a single founder cell up to large colonies in reasonable runtime.
Another significant speedup was achieved by using PyVista for the visualisation of the cell colony. The speedup compared to matplotlib for visualising a large number of cells (up to half a million), including specular lighting, is truly impressive.

Inducing morphology: Reaction-Diffusion calculations

      Morphogenesis is the process through which a lump of cells develop various shapes. Alan Turing's pioneering work on the topic, presented in the seminal 1952 paper "The chemical basis of morphogenesis", showed that the diffusion of two chemical signals, one acting as an activator of growth and the other as a deactivator can lead naturally to complex patterns, now know as Turing pattern. Since then, several biological so-called 'morphogens' have been discovered that act as promoters or inhibitors of cell growth, and lead to a wide range of patterning throughout life, from the appearance of fingers on limbs to zebra's stripes, and beyond. One morphogen that plays a crucial role in animal morphogenesis is the Sonic hedgehog protein (it's really called that way).
In order to generate Turing patterns in CellGrow, and to check whether it can lead to some interesting morphologies, I have implemented the Gray-Scott model: \[\begin{aligned}\frac{\partial u}{\partial t}&=\nabla^2 u+u^2v - (a+b)u,\\ \frac{\partial v}{\partial t}&=D\nabla^2v-u^2v + a(1 - v),\end{aligned}\] where \(u\) and \(v\) are assigned to each cell, and the coefficients \(a\) and \(b\) control various patterning behaviours. Diffusion takes place through averaging across neighbouring cells, and advancing in time uses Euler's method.
While researching on the Gray-Scott model, I have stumbled upon the incredibly useful and user-friendly PDE solver and visalizer website called VisualPDE, which showcases tens of interesting two-dimensional PDEs across various areas of science, including the Gray-Scott model. This helped me to reality-check the validity of my model, which instead of being calculated on a fixed two-dimensional grid, it is calculated across neighbouring points in 3D space. Remarkably, there is a high degree of correspondence in patterning between the 2D model on VisualPDE and what I observe evolving across the cells, that helped me guide the process.

Once the reaction-diffusion variables are inside cells and evolving, there are a myriad options to choose from what to do with them: we can make division, statis, apoptosis, polarity, and so on, depend on the variables \(u\) and \(v\). I have played around with turning these knobs more than I wish to admit, but I was also rewarded, as you will see below, with some very interesting patterns. Additionally, we can make polarity align between cells to various degrees, the alignment strength depend on the reaction-diffusion variables, and furthermore, to have the relaxation step only act along the polarity of each cell. The possibilies on how these various dynamics interact are endless, leading to a vast parameter space to be explored in order to find interesting dynamics.
